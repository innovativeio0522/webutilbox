GIF Maker - Create Animated GIFs from Images

The GIF Maker lets you create animated GIFs from a series of images directly in your browser. Upload your frames, arrange them in the desired order, set the delay and transition effects, and export a looping animated GIF — no software installation required and no images uploaded to any server.

What Is GIF Maker?

The GIF Maker is a browser-based animation tool that takes a sequence of still images and combines them into an animated GIF. You upload multiple image files, which become the individual frames of your animation. The tool displays them in a reorderable list so you can drag frames into the exact sequence you want before generating the final animation.

GIF (Graphics Interchange Format) is an image format that supports animation by storing multiple frames in a single file. Each frame is displayed for a set duration before advancing to the next, creating the illusion of motion. GIFs loop continuously by default and are universally supported across all platforms, messaging apps, social media, email clients, and web browsers — no video player or codec is required to view them.

This tool goes beyond basic frame sequencing by offering transition effects between frames. You can choose from instant cuts, cross-fades, slide transitions in four directions, and zoom effects. A reverse loop option plays the animation forward then backward for a seamless ping-pong effect, and a bounce easing option adds smooth acceleration and deceleration to transitions for a more polished result.

Key Features

  • Multi-image upload with drag-and-drop reordering of frames, so you can arrange your sequence in any order before generating the animation.
  • Configurable frame delay in milliseconds, letting you control the speed of the animation from fast (50ms) to slow (5000ms) per frame.
  • Eight transition effects: None (instant cut), Fade, Slide Left, Slide Right, Slide Up, Slide Down, Zoom In, and Zoom Out.
  • Configurable transition duration separate from frame delay, giving you independent control over how long each transition takes.
  • Reverse loop option that plays the animation forward then backward for a seamless ping-pong effect without needing duplicate frames.
  • Bounce easing option that applies ease-in/ease-out timing to transitions for smoother, more natural-looking motion.

How to Use GIF Maker

Creating an animated GIF from your images takes just a few steps and produces a preview you can review before downloading.

  • Step 1: Click "Select Images" and choose two or more image files from your device — PNG, JPG, and other common formats are supported.
  • Step 2: Review the frame list and drag frames to reorder them if needed to get the sequence right.
  • Step 3: Configure the GIF settings: set the frame delay, choose a transition effect and duration, and set the maximum width.
  • Step 4: Enable optional effects like Loop, Reverse Loop, or Bounce as desired for your animation style.
  • Step 5: Click "Create GIF" to generate the animation, preview it in the preview panel, and click "Download GIF" to save it.

Tips and Best Practices

The most important factor for GIF quality is keeping the image dimensions small. GIF files grow quickly with size — a 500px wide GIF can be several times larger than a 250px wide version of the same animation. Resize your source images to the smallest dimensions that still look good before uploading them. The Max Width setting in the tool lets you cap the output size without needing to pre-resize your images.

GIF supports a maximum of 256 colors per frame, which means photographic images with smooth gradients and many colors will look noticeably degraded in GIF format. GIF works best for images with flat colors, simple graphics, illustrations, and screenshots with limited color palettes. For photographic content, consider using WebP animations or short MP4 videos instead, which offer dramatically better quality at smaller file sizes.

Frame count and frame delay have a direct tradeoff with file size and smoothness. More frames at shorter delays produce smoother animation but larger files. For most purposes, 10-15 frames per second (100-67ms delay) strikes a good balance. For simple animations with clear distinct states, fewer frames with longer delays can be just as effective and produce much smaller files.
