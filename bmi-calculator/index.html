What Is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from a person's weight and height. It is calculated by dividing weight in kilograms by the square of height in meters (kg/m²). For imperial measurements, the formula is weight in pounds divided by height in inches squared, multiplied by 703. The resulting number is used to categorize individuals into weight status groups that are associated with different levels of health risk.

BMI was developed in the 19th century by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet and has been used as a population-level health screening tool for decades. The World Health Organization (WHO) and health agencies worldwide use BMI thresholds to define underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obesity categories. These categories are associated with statistical risks for conditions like cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and certain cancers.

It is important to understand that BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnostic measure. It does not directly measure body fat percentage, muscle mass, bone density, or fat distribution. A person with a high BMI may be healthy if they have high muscle mass, and a person with a normal BMI may still have health risks if they carry excess fat around the abdomen. BMI should always be interpreted in the context of a broader health assessment.

BMI Categories and What They Mean

The standard BMI categories used by the World Health Organization provide a general framework for assessing weight status and associated health risks.

  • Underweight (BMI below 18.5) — may indicate nutritional deficiency, malnutrition, or underlying health conditions. Associated with risks including weakened immune function, bone loss, and anemia.
  • Normal weight (BMI 18.5–24.9) — generally associated with the lowest risk of weight-related health conditions. This range is considered healthy for most adults.
  • Overweight (BMI 25–29.9) — associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and hypertension. Lifestyle changes like diet and exercise can help reduce risk.
  • Obese Class I (BMI 30–34.9) — associated with significantly increased health risks. Medical guidance is recommended for weight management.
  • Obese Class II (BMI 35–39.9) — high risk category with substantially elevated risk of serious health conditions.
  • Obese Class III (BMI 40 and above) — very high risk, sometimes called severe or morbid obesity. Medical intervention is typically recommended.

Tips and Best Practices

For the most accurate BMI calculation, use precise measurements rather than estimates. Weigh yourself in the morning before eating, without shoes or heavy clothing, using a calibrated scale. Measure your height without shoes, standing straight against a wall. Small errors in height measurement can have a noticeable effect on BMI because height is squared in the formula.

Keep in mind that BMI has known limitations for certain populations. Athletes and bodybuilders often have a high BMI due to muscle mass rather than excess fat, which can incorrectly classify them as overweight or obese. Conversely, older adults who have lost muscle mass may have a normal BMI despite having a high body fat percentage. For these groups, additional measures like waist circumference, body fat percentage, or DEXA scans provide a more accurate picture of health.

BMI thresholds may also differ by ethnicity. Research has shown that people of Asian descent may have higher health risks at lower BMI values compared to people of European descent. Some health organizations recommend lower BMI thresholds for Asian populations. If you are of Asian descent, consult your healthcare provider about the appropriate BMI thresholds for your background.
