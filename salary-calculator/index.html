What Is a Salary Calculator?

A salary calculator is a tool that converts compensation figures between different time periods and calculates the impact of taxes and deductions on your actual take-home pay. Most job offers are quoted as either an hourly rate or an annual salary, but understanding what that means on a weekly or monthly basis — and what you'll actually receive after deductions — requires several calculations that this tool handles automatically.

This calculator goes beyond simple conversion. It accounts for overtime pay at a configurable multiplier, annual bonuses, estimated tax rates, and monthly deductions like health insurance or retirement contributions. The result is a comprehensive view of your total compensation and what portion of it you actually keep.

All calculations are performed instantly in your browser as you adjust any input. There's no need to click a calculate button — the results update in real time, making it easy to explore different scenarios like "what if I work 5 hours of overtime per week?" or "how much does a 5% raise change my monthly take-home?"

Tips and Best Practices

For the most accurate net pay estimate, use your effective tax rate rather than your marginal rate. Your effective rate is your total tax paid divided by your total income — it's typically lower than your marginal (top bracket) rate. You can find your effective rate on last year's tax return. Remember that this calculator provides an estimate; your actual withholding may differ based on filing status, deductions, and other factors.

When evaluating job offers, don't forget to factor in benefits as part of total compensation. Health insurance, retirement matching, paid time off, and other perks have real monetary value. A job with a slightly lower salary but better benefits may actually be worth more in total compensation than a higher-paying offer with minimal benefits.

If you're a freelancer or contractor, set your weeks per year to account for unpaid time off, slow periods, and time spent on non-billable work. Using 48 or 46 weeks instead of 52 gives a more realistic picture of your actual annual earnings from billable hours.
