What Is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest, which is calculated only on the original principal, compound interest grows exponentially over time because each period's interest is added to the principal before the next period's interest is calculated. This self-reinforcing growth mechanism is often described as "interest on interest" and is the fundamental principle behind long-term wealth accumulation.

The mathematical formula for compound interest is A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where A is the final amount, P is the principal, r is the annual interest rate expressed as a decimal, n is the number of times interest compounds per year, and t is the time in years. This formula reveals that all four variables — principal, rate, compounding frequency, and time — contribute to the final result, but time has a uniquely powerful effect because it appears as an exponent. Doubling the time period does not double the final amount; it squares the growth factor, producing dramatically larger results.

Albert Einstein is often credited with calling compound interest the "eighth wonder of the world," though the attribution is disputed. Regardless of its origin, the sentiment captures a genuine mathematical truth: the exponential nature of compound growth produces results that are deeply counterintuitive to human linear thinking. A modest investment made early in life can grow to a sum that dwarfs a much larger investment made later, simply because of the additional years of compounding. This is why financial advisors consistently emphasize starting to save as early as possible, even with small amounts.

The compounding frequency — how often interest is calculated and added to the principal — also affects the final result, though less dramatically than time or rate. Daily compounding produces slightly more than monthly compounding, which produces slightly more than annual compounding. The difference between daily and annual compounding on the same principal and rate is typically a few percent over long periods, which can still represent a meaningful amount of money on large investments.

How to Use the Compound Interest Calculator

Calculating your investment growth takes just a few seconds with no financial expertise required.

  • Step 1: Enter your principal amount — the initial lump sum you are investing or have already invested.
  • Step 2: Enter the annual interest rate as a percentage — for example, 7 for a 7% annual return.

Tips and Best Practices

When using this calculator for retirement planning, use a conservative interest rate assumption to avoid overestimating your future wealth. Historical stock market returns have averaged around 7% annually after inflation, but individual years vary widely. Using 5% to 6% as your assumed rate of return gives you a more conservative projection that is less likely to disappoint. Always run multiple scenarios with different rates to understand the range of possible outcomes.

The monthly contribution feature is one of the most powerful aspects of this calculator. Even small regular contributions have a dramatic effect on the final balance over long time periods, because each contribution also benefits from compounding for the remainder of the investment period. Try comparing a scenario with a large lump sum and no contributions against one with a smaller lump sum and modest monthly contributions — you may be surprised by how competitive the regular contribution approach is over 20 or 30 years.

Pay attention to the year-by-year table to understand the "hockey stick" shape of compound growth. In the early years, the growth appears modest and linear. But in the later years, the annual interest earned can exceed the original principal, and the balance accelerates rapidly. This visualization helps build the intuition for why time is the most valuable variable in compound interest — every year you delay investing is a year of exponential growth you cannot recover.
